Calculation of radiation stresses¶

Momentum Balance at the wave-averaged scale

The x and y-direction momentum balances can be examined using the concept of radiation stresses introduced by Longuet-Higgins and Stewart (1964). The major terms in the momentum balances can be presented by the wave-averaged, depth-integrated shallow water equations for time-steady flow,

()¶\[\]

frac{partial bar{P}bar{P}/H}{partial x} + frac{partial bar{P}bar{Q}/H}{partial y}+ gHfrac{partial bar{eta}}{partial x}+ frac{partial S_{xx}}{partial x} + frac{partial S_{xy}}{partial y} + gHfrac{partial bar{eta}}{partial x} + bar{tau}_x+ R_x =0,

()¶\[\]

frac{partial bar{Q}bar{Q}/H}{partial y} + frac{partial bar{P}bar{Q}/H}{partial x}+ gHfrac{partial bar{eta}}{partial y}+ frac{partial S_{yy}}{partial y} + frac{partial S_{xy}}{partial x} + gHfrac{partial bar{eta}}{partial y} + bar{tau}_y+ R_y =0,

where \(\bar{()}\) represents wave-averaging. \(H = h + \bar{\eta}\), is the wave-averaged water depth with \(h\) and \(\eta\) representing the still water depth and surface elevation, respectively. In the Cartesian coordinates (\(x,y\)), \((\bar{P}, \bar{Q})\) are wave-averaged volume fluxes in (\(x,y\)) directions. (\(\bar{\tau}_x, \bar{\tau}_y\) represent the bottom friction. \(g\) is the gravitational acceleration constant. The calculation of radiation stresses \(S_{xx} \), and \(S_{xy}\) is desribed below. In the equations above, \(R_x\) and \(R_y\) are residual momentum terms in the momentum balances in \(x-\) and \(y-\) directions, respectively.

Calculation of radiation stresses

The radiation stresses can be expressed by

()¶\[\]

S_{xx} = overline{int_{-h}^eta (u^2-w^2) dz } + frac{1}{2} g overline{eta^2},

()¶\[\]

S_{yy} = overline{int_{-h}^eta (v^2-w^2) dz } + frac{1}{2} g overline{eta^2},

()¶\[\]

S_{xy} = overline{int_{-h}^eta uv dz },

where \((u,v,w)\) represent velocity components in \((x,y,z)\) directions. In this study, the velocity components were estimated by modeled velocity field accurate up to \(O(\mu^2)\). To make it convenient to remove wave-averaged current velocity from the instantaneous velocity, we used the depth-averaged velocity \(\tilde{\bf u} = \bf{u}_\alpha + {\bf \bar{u}}_2\), in which \(\bf{u}_\alpha\) denotes the velocity at a reference elevation and \({\bf \bar{u}}_2\) is the depth averaged \(O(\mu^2)\) contribution to the horizontal velocity field. The vertical component \(w\) was estimated by \(w(x,y,z,t) = - \mu^2 \nabla \cdot [(h+z) {\bf u}_{\alpha}]\). For the specific application in this study, the depth-averaged vertical velocity \(\tilde{w}\) was roughly estimated as half of the vertical velocity at the surface, i.e., \(\tilde{w} = w(x,y,\eta,t)/2\). The final expressions of radiation stresses are

()¶\[S_{xx} = \overline{[(\tilde{u}-\tilde{u}_m)^2 - \tilde{w}^2] (h+\eta)} +\frac{1}{2} g \overline{\eta^2},\]
()¶\[S_{yy} = \overline{[(\tilde{v}-\tilde{v}_m)^2 - \tilde{w}^2] (h+\eta)} +\frac{1}{2} g \overline{\eta^2},\]
()¶\[S_{xy} = \overline{(\tilde{u}-\tilde{u}_m)(\tilde{v}-\tilde{v}_m) (h+\eta)},\]

where (\(\tilde{u}_m, \tilde{v}_m\)) represent the wave-averaged, depth-averaged velocities in (\(x,y\)) directions. It should be mentioned that the ordering of the terms in the radiation stresses above is not exactly consistent with the derivation of the fully nonlinear Boussinesq model citep{wei_1995}. Errors may exist in \(O(\mu^4)\) contributions.

Calculations of time averaged quantities (\(\tilde{u}_m, \tilde{v}_m\)), \((\bar{P}, \bar{Q})\) and the radiation stresses, \(S_{xx}$, $S_{xy}\), \(S_{yy}\), were integrated in specified time segments, such as 20 wave periods. In the calculations of the instantaneous wave velocity, such as \(\tilde{u} - \tilde{u}_m\), the averaged value \(\tilde{u}_m\) is from the averaging in the last time segment.

For spectral wave simulations, both average wave height and root-mean-square (RMS) wave height are calculated using zero-crossing at each grid point. Significant wave height is evaluated using Goda’s (2000) formula:

()¶\[H_{1/3} = 4.004\sqrt{m_0}\]

where,

()¶\[m_0 = \frac{1}{t_2-t_1} \int^{t_2}_{t_1} \eta^2 dt\]
